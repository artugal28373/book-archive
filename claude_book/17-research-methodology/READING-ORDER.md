# Research Methodology — Reading Order

## Path

These are short — read all three early in your research career, not after.

1. **How to Read a Paper** — S. Keshav (~10 pages)
   The three-pass method. Read this BEFORE attempting your first research paper. Will save you weeks.

2. **The Researcher's Bible** — Bundy
   Practical advice on doing PhD-level CS research: choosing problems, doing literature reviews, managing supervisors.

3. **Mathematical Writing** — Knuth/Larrabee/Roberts
   Knuth's lecture notes on writing CS/math papers. A bit dated but the principles still apply.

## What's missing (PAID — worth buying)

- **Writing for Computer Science (3e)** — Justin Zobel ⭐ (the most practical book on CS academic writing)
- **The Craft of Research (4e)** — Booth/Colomb/Williams (research process, broader than CS)
- **The Elements of Style** — Strunk/White (general writing)

## Free external resources
- **The PhD Grind** — Philip Guo (free memoir at pgbovine.net) — what doing a CS PhD actually looks like
- **Patrick Winston's How to Speak** — MIT lecture (YouTube, free) — best 60 minutes on academic presentation
- **Writing the Methods Section** — various university writing centers

## Workflow tools (worth learning early)
- **LaTeX** + Overleaf for writing
- **Zotero** for reference management
- **Obsidian / Notion** for note-taking
- **arxiv-sanity-lite** + Connected Papers + Semantic Scholar for finding papers

## Habits
- Read 2-3 papers/week minimum
- Keep paper notes (1-2 paragraphs each: question, method, result, what I learned)
- Reproduce one paper end-to-end every quarter
- Write at least one summary blog post per month — forces you to articulate
